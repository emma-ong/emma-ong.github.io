Problem & Solution

Sprint 4's JS Kata exercise: Built-in Methods, whereAreYou. I was stuck on this problem for a long time as I could not come to understand what the task listed in the console was looking for. I soon realised that there were instructions in the EDA handbook and a massive hint for what method the challenge was after. I was only looking at the hints in the console before this, not realising there was more information to base off of. Additionally, the challenge at hand was suggesting to use a new method, one that I had not used before, so I googled and used the MDN Wed Docs as a primary resource to understand the syntax (the structure of the method). I still had to look at different resources such as w3schools and javascripttutorial to fully grasp how it may be applied to different situations. I then tested my understanding on the console to see what results it returned and after a many error messages, it made alot more sense. After multiple rounds of hair pulling, I finally got it to run!

It was definitely a frustrating moment and the fault was all mine of course! The moment I realised I missed reading the main point of the task, it was a mix of hooray! and ðŸ¤¦.

Lesson learnt

I could have saved alot of time had I read through the instructions beforehand. Strange, as I am normally someone who tends towards over-reading before jumping into things and one of my goals to achieve at the end of the bootcamp, is to do be more selective in what I read and invest more time in action. However, in this case, it somewhat backfired. Moving forward, I will try find a balance between the two. Lesson has been learnt!

Problem Solving Techniques I use:

Pseudocode
I find pseudocode extremely helpful in structuring what path to take when writing code and a point of reference when returning back to code that was written in the past. Especially when I am learning a new concept or have struggled with something, I ensure to break down the steps in a clear and concise way because there would be a high chance I forget how to do it later down the line.

Trying something, Console.logging, Reading error messages & Googling
I often do a test run the console to gauge my core understanding of a piece of code and this has been part of my process thus far. I have yet to fully understand the error messages when I see them prima facie, however a quick google or search on stackoverflow tends to clarify them.

Rubber ducky method
While I have often used this process as a means to help me understand something, I did not realise there was a coined term for it, until now! I do find myself talking outloud to myself when I have reached a roadblock and while I write my psuedocode out.

Asking your peers for help & Asking coaches for help
I am comfortable asking questions and seeking for help when needed. I anticipate there will be many questions coming once bootcamp begins!

Improving your process with reflection I do find that reflective writing has helped me pay more attention to my strengths, weaknesses and goals during this time. I find prompts a good starting point to dump my thoughts and refine what I aim to convey in a manner that is honest, concise and constructive.
